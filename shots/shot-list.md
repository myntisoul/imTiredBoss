# Shot ID: SH01

## Oracle's Office

**Purpose:** Establishes the visual language for all scenes with the Oracle. A key location that shows the contrast between a supernatural entity and its deliberately mundane, "human" disguise. The room should feel both cozy and oppressive, familiar and alien.

**Scene:** A dream space. The protagonist stands in a doorway leading into absolute, impenetrable darkness. He takes a step — and finds himself in the office. A small room bathed in warm but dim yellow light from a single green glass-shaded desk lamp. Dense shadows fill the corners, never fully dispersing — they seem almost *alive*, subtly shifting at the edge of vision. The air is dusty, heavy, as if untouched for decades.

**Camera:** View from the doorway (the protagonist's entry point). The room is seen slightly from the side, creating a sense of observation. The Oracle's figure sits at the desk in half-profile, face partially hidden in shadow.

**Must-have:**
- **Overall atmosphere:** "Frozen time." The room should look as if untouched for fifty years, yet not abandoned — cared for, lived in.
- **Desk:** Massive, dark wood, worn, with ink stains. On it: a green glass lamp (the only light source), stacks of yellowed papers, a fountain pen, an inkwell. The Oracle writes — slowly, carefully, without looking up.
- **Bookshelves:** Floor-to-ceiling along one wall. Old books with leather spines and faded titles. Between books — random objects: an old photo frame (turned face to the wall), a dried flower, mechanical stopped clocks.
- **Gramophone:** On a separate stand against the wall. Large brass horn. Stack of records in dusty sleeves nearby. Not playing, but feels like it would play something very old and sad if wound up.
- **Oracle (the shell):** Man, 50-55, nondescript, "plain." Worn gray jacket, white shirt without tie, tired eyes behind thin metal-frame glasses. Slightly balding, hands careful but weary. Doesn't look up immediately — keeps writing, letting the protagonist realize where he is.
- **Floor:** Old parquet, creaky in places, worn to bare wood in others. In shadows near walls — what looks like dampness or stains, but upon closer inspection, it's *something dark* that doesn't want to be looked at.

**Nice-to-have:**
- Wall map of the city — but marked with strange symbols or lines not found in reality.
- Grandfather clock in corner. Pendulum doesn't move, but clock ticks. Or vice versa — pendulum moves, but absolute silence.
- Window (if present) — looks out onto the same darkness as beyond the door. Sometimes a silhouette passes by, but vanishes when looked at directly.

**Avoid:**
- **No "divine" aesthetics.** No candles (except the lamp), no religious symbols, halos, floating objects, mystical glows. The Oracle chose this form deliberately — to be *grounded*.
- **No high-tech.** No computers, modern lamps, plastic, neon. Technology stopped somewhere in the mid-20th century.
- **No "horror" room.** Not a haunted location. It should be *unsettling* precisely because of its ordinariness. Not monsters in corners, but the feeling you shouldn't be here — yet you were invited.
- **Oracle must not look sinister.** No glowing eyes, no demonic features. He's a clerk, a tired bureaucrat. His power shows not in appearance, but in how the room *obeys* him (walls can shift, lights die if he wills it).
- **No clutter.** Not messy. Everything in its place, even dust lies evenly. A *controlled* space, despite its old-fashioned look.

## Protagonist in His Apartment

**Purpose:** Establishes the protagonist's visual identity and living environment. Shows who he is before the Oracle's intervention — a broken man holding onto the last threads of dignity.

**Scene:** An old apartment from the 1990s–2000s, the kind a factory worker could afford. Faded yellow and gray wallpaper peeling at the corners. Worn wooden furniture, nothing matching, nothing new. On the bed sits a man in his forties. He's not lying down — he's sitting upright, but hunched, as if the weight on his shoulders won't let him straighten his back. His hands hold a cigarette, almost finished, ash threatening to fall. His face shows deep anguish: not the performative pain of tragedy, but the quiet, exhausted look of someone who has stopped hoping but hasn't stopped hurting. His body bears scars — some old, some fresher — visible on his arms. He's dressed in a plain white t-shirt and jeans, clean but worn. Despite everything, he maintains a semblance of order: he's washed, his clothes are clean, he hasn't let go completely. Beside him, a small table holds a half-empty bottle of vodka and a Schofield revolver. The revolver isn't pointed at him, just sitting there, present, an option kept open.

**Camera:** Medium shot, slightly angled. We see the protagonist on the bed, the table with the revolver in the foreground, almost between him and the viewer. The room around him is soft focus — what matters is the man and the weight he carries.

**Must-have:**
- **The protagonist:** Male, early 40s. Lean, not frail — factory work has given him physical strength, but now it's fading. Short, unkempt hair, stubble. Eyes that have seen too much but still blink. White t-shirt, simple jeans, no shoes. Scars on forearms (visible), possibly one on the collarbone peeking from the t-shirt. Cigarette in one hand, the other resting on his knee, fingers slightly trembling.
- **The face:** Not handsome in a conventional way. Deep lines around the mouth, dark circles under the eyes. The expression is key: not anger, not even sadness — a mixture of *fear, fatigue, and the last residue of will*. He's still here. He hasn't given up completely, even though he's close.
- **The apartment:** Small, cramped. Yellow-gray wallpaper, stained in places. Old wooden furniture: a wardrobe with a missing handle, a nightstand, a table. One window with cheap curtains, letting in grey light. No decorations, no personal touches except the essentials.
- **The table:** Small, rickety. A bottle of vodka (cheap brand, half-empty, no glass — he drinks from the bottle). A Schofield revolver, old but maintained, placed not hidden but simply *there*, as part of the furniture. Ashtray overflowing with cigarette butts.
- **Lighting:** Grey, muted. Daylight from the window is weak, overcast. No warm lamps here. The room feels cold, damp, unloved.
- **Tone:** Quiet desperation. This is not a dramatic suicide setup, not a movie scene. This is a Tuesday. This is a man who has been sitting like this for hours, days, weeks. The gun is not raised — it's just sitting. That's more frightening than any dramatic gesture.

**Nice-to-have:**
- A factory uniform hanging on a hook behind the door. Dusty, unworn for some time.
- Empty medicine packets on the nightstand (cheap sedatives, painkillers).
- A child's drawing tucked into the frame of a cheap mirror on the wall. Not displayed prominently, just *kept*.
- An old radio on the windowsill, silent.
- The bed is made. Even in this state, he makes the bed. This small detail speaks louder than any dialogue.

**Avoid:**
- **No "cool" aesthetic.** He's not a tortured hero, not a John Wick. No dramatic lighting, no noir shadows. This is not stylish suffering — it's ugly, mundane, real.
- **No excessive filth.** The apartment is worn, but not a garbage dump. He's not a hoarder, he's not living in squalor. He maintains basic hygiene and order. His tragedy is internal, not external chaos.
- **No overt suicide staging.** The gun is on the table, not in his hand, not pointed at his head. It's a presence, a possibility, not an active threat in this moment.
- **No dramatic poses.** He's not staring into the distance contemplating life. He's just sitting. Maybe staring at the floor, at the cigarette, at nothing. The stillness should feel heavy, not cinematic.
- **No "handsome" casting.** The protagonist is ordinary. Not ugly, but not memorable. He looks like any man who worked a factory job for twenty years and had the life slowly drained out of him.
- **No warm colors.** No golden hour light, no cozy warmth. The palette is grey, beige, faded yellow, worn brown. Life has leached out of this space.

## Scene in Mansion

**Purpose:** One of the most important scenes in the game. A turning point where two opposing protagonists meet face to face. Establishes the moral weight of the story and the human cost behind the gameplay.  
**Scene:** The ground floor of a wealthy man's mansion. The protagonist has just finished a full sweep — bodies lie scattered, blood pools on expensive floors, discarded weapons rest against overturned furniture. The air is thick with gunpowder and death. In the center of the room, the protagonist stands with a young girl's hand in his. She is the victim, the one locked in the basement. His white t-shirt is now smeared with blood — not his own. His face is hollow, exhausted, the adrenaline fading into something heavier. His eyes show not rage, but _fear and uncertainty_. He wears black fingerless gloves. Beside him, the girl moves as if sleepwalking. Her legs barely carry her. Her face is blank — the hollow emptiness after all tears have been used. She is not crying because there is nothing left. Across the room, an elite police squad has him cornered. Muzzles are raised, laser sights tremble on his chest. The officer leads them — a woman in her thirties, tactical gear, face hardened by years of service. But behind the professional composure, something else flickers: _fear of what she might have to do, and hesitation she cannot fully suppress_. She shouts at him to release the child. One wrong move and the room erupts.  
**Camera:** Wide shot, low angle, positioned slightly behind the officer's squad. We see the full tableau: the armed team in focus in the foreground, the protagonist and the girl isolated in the background, framed by the chaos of the room. The distance between them feels both physical and moral.  
**Must-have:**

- **The protagonist:** The same man from his apartment, but transformed by violence. White t-shirt now stained with blood (splatter, not his). Jeans darkened with dirt and fluid. Black fingerless gloves, worn from use. Face: exhaustion deeper than sleep can fix. Eyes wide but not wild — he is _present_, he knows what he's done, and he is terrified of what comes next. He holds the girl's hand not as a shield, but as someone who is afraid to let go of the one thing he saved. His posture is not aggressive — shoulders slightly dropped, weight on back foot. He is not a threat in this moment, but the blood on him says otherwise.
- **The girl:** 8–10 years old. Dirty, underfed, wearing clothes that are too big (likely provided by her captor). Face: the emptiness of prolonged trauma. Eyes open but not seeing. Dried tear tracks on her cheeks. She is not clinging to the protagonist — she is simply there, too broken to resist, too exhausted to hope. Her hand in his is limp. She stands because her legs haven't given out yet. She is the moral fulcrum of the scene.
- **The officer:** Female, early 30s. Fit, professional. Tactical vest, helmet, assault rifle raised and aimed. Her stance is textbook — but her face tells another story. There is _resolve_ — she knows her duty. But there is also _fear_ — of the blood-soaked man before her, of the child in his grip, of the shot she may have to take. Her finger is on the trigger guard, not the trigger — a small detail showing she has not yet made the decision to fire. Her mouth is open, mid-command. Sweat on her brow.
- **The squad:** 3–4 operators in tactical gear, spread out, taking cover behind overturned furniture and doorframes. Their weapons are aimed. They are professional — no panic, just waiting for the order.
- **The room:** Upstairs mansion — expensive furniture, chandeliers, artwork on walls. Now torn apart by the protagonist's rampage. Bodies (suggested, not graphic) in the background: guards, staff, the owner. Weapons scattered. Bullet holes in walls, glass shattered. Blood on the marble floor. The contrast between wealth and violence is deliberate.  
    **Nice-to-have:**
- A large window behind the protagonist, letting in grey morning light. He and the girl are backlit, silhouetted slightly — their faces in shadow, but their outlines clear.
- The officer's hand on the rifle trembles almost imperceptibly. A detail that shows her conflict without words.
- The girl's hand in the protagonist's — her fingers do not grip back. His hand is the only thing keeping her upright.
- One of the squad members has lowered their weapon slightly, just a fraction. Not following protocol. Human reaction to the girl.
- A family photograph on the wall behind the officer — intact, untouched. The owner's family. Irony of normalcy.  
    **Avoid:**
- **No glorification of violence.** The bodies are not "cool." They are messy, wrong, heavy. The scene should feel like a crime scene, not a cover shoot.
- **No heroic framing of the protagonist.** He is not a antihero striking a dramatic pose. He is a man who has done terrible things and is now trapped. Sympathetic? Possibly. Heroic? No.
- **No villainous framing of the officer.** She is not a cold killer. Her hesitation is not weakness — it's humanity. The scene should make the player understand _both_ of them.
- **No overly dramatic lighting.** No shafts of divine light, no noir shadows. Grey morning light, flat and unforgiving. This is not a fantasy — it's reality at its heaviest.
- **The girl must not look "cute" or "precious."** She is a trauma victim, not a storybook innocent. Her emptiness should be painful to look at.
- **No excessive gore.** Blood is present, but the bodies are suggested, not lingered on. The focus is on the living.
- **No clear visual cues for who is "right."** The scene should not tell the player who to side with. The tension should remain unresolved.
